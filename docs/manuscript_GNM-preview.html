Introduction

Nest and nest-building are fundamental across a diverse range of animals, including arthropods (e.g., termites, spiders), mammals (e.g., platypus, rodents), and perhaps more famously, birds. Nests provide structural support for eggs, parents, and offspring, and protect them against predators and environmental pressures, and are thus key to reproductive success and survival (Hansell and Overhill 2000; Mainwaring et al. 2014; Deeming 2023). While most birds use organic plant materials such as twigs, branches, grass, and moss, some incorporate inorganic items, including mud, stones, feathers, snake skins and even anthropogenic materials like plastic and cigarette butts (Sergio et al. 2011; Suárez-Rodríguez, López-Rull, and Macías Garcia 2013; Sheard et al. 2024). These are considered non-structural additions and suggest that nests are more than just physical shelters; they can be complex tools shaped by both natural and sexual selection for multiple functions. Indeed, nests can play a key role in sexual display, and features such as size have been shown to act as sexually selected signals of parental quality in several bird species (Moreno et al. 1994; HOI, SCHLEICHER, and VALERA 1996), even interspecifically (Soler et al. 1995). Nest-building behaviour is increasingly recognized as an expression of the extended phenotype (reviewed by Moreno (2012)), and phylogeny has been shown to be an important factor explaining both mean and within-species variation in nest morphology (Perez, Manica, and Medina 2023). Yet, despite hundreds of scientific articles studying nests and nesting behaviour in birds being published each year, much remains unknown.

An intriguing nesting behaviour observed in some bird species is the addition of fresh, often aromatic, plant material to the nest. This green nest material (hereafter GNM), which often corresponds to leaves, sprigs, herbs and branches, is typically added from the start of nest building until egg laying, although some species like the blue tit (Cyanistes cyanus), keep adding it even after the nest is finished. Importantly, contrary to the structural nest materials (e.g., twigs, mud), GNM is selected from a small, non-random subset of local flora (Clark and Mason 1985; Petit et al. 2002; Garrido-Bautista et al. 2023), and often characterized by high levels of aromatic volatile compounds (commonly used by songbirds: Mennerat, Mirleau, et al. (2009)) or secondary metabolites such as oils and resins (commonly used by raptors: Ontiveros, Caro, and Pleguezuelos (2007)). GNM is unlikely to be gathered opportunistically, and collecting these uncommon GNM, and replenishing it for some times weeks, requires extra time, and in some cases flying outside one’s territory (Mennerat, Perret, and Lambrechts 2009). Thus, the general costs associated with nest-building (Mainwaring and Hartley 2013), and the additional effort, time and energy devoted to add these non-structural materials suggest an important adaptive function of GNM.

Several hypotheses have been proposed to explain the adaptive function of GNM (reviewed in Dubiec, Góźdź, and Mazgajski (2013); Scott-Baumann and Morgan (2015)) First, the courtship hypothesis suggests that (mostly) males use GNM as visual or olfactory signals to attract females and/or advertise nest site occupancy to other males (e.g., Eens, Pinxten, and Verheyen (1993); Helga Gwinner (1997); Brouwer and Komdeur (2004))), particularly in cavity-nesting species where visual cues inside the nest are limited. The courtship hypothesis traces back to the early natural history observations by Kessel (1957), who noted that male European Starlings (Sturnus vulgaris) carried fresh plant material to their nests while singing and displaying to females. Subsequent studies in both European and Spotless Starlings (Sturnus unicolor) supported this view that GNM may serve as a sexually selected signal of male quality by showing that females often prefer nests with fresh GNM, and that unpaired males conspicuously deliver GNM during courtship, stopping right after the onset of egg laying (Brouwer and Komdeur 2004; Veiga, Polo, and Viñuela 2006), and increasing this behaviour in the presence of potential mates (Eens, Pinxten, and Verheyen 1993) The courtship hypothesis was later extended to explain GNM addition solely by females in Blue Tits, therefore suggesting that GNM might serve as a sexually selected signal of female quality (Tomás et al. 2013).

Second, the most widely tested hypothesis is the nest protection hypothesis, which proposes that the volatile compounds in GNM reduce the amount of parasites and pathogens in the nests, enhancing offspring survival. This hypothesis traces back to a comparative study in raptors (Falconiformes) that provided indirect evidence for it by comparing nest reuse, diet composition and GNM presence (Wimberger 1984). Soon after, a preference for GNM containing insect-repellent and antimicrobial volatiles was experimentally confirmed in European Starlings, therefore confirming two of the three key criteria for evaluating the nest protection hypothesis (i.e., selective plant use, bioactive chemical properties and demonstrable adaptive benefits; Clark and Mason 1988). Observational and experimental studies on raptors (Ontiveros, Caro, and Pleguezuelos 2007; Dykstra, Hays, and Simon 2009) and passerines (Lafuma, Lambrechts, and Raymond 2001; Dawson 2004; Mennerat, Mirleau, et al. 2009; Quiroga, Reboreda, and Beltzer 2012) have shown mixed results. In some cases, GNM was shown to improve offspring condition without reducing the amount of parasites in the nests (H. Gwinner et al. 2000), which led to the development of the drug hypothesis. This alternative hypothesis suggests that the volatiles present in GNM directly enhance offspring immune function and/or health independent of parasite or pathogen control (H. Gwinner et al. 2000; Mennerat, Perret, et al. 2009). Thus, while both the nest protection and drug hypothesis suggest an adaptive benefit of GNM by enhancing offspring survival, the drug hypothesis emphasizes a pharmacological mechanism, whereas the nest protection hypothesis emphasizes parasite and pathogen repellency. Importantly, these two hypotheses are not mutually exclusive unless the improvement in offspring health can be proved to be unrelated to a reduction in parasite or pathogen load, something difficult to achieve.

Last, several additional adaptive hypotheses or functions of GNM that do not refer to its aromatic (i.e., volatiles and secondary metabolites) properties have been also proposed. These include benefits such as enhancing the crypticity of the nest (Skutch 1957), providing insulation or sun protection for eggs and offspring (Mertens 1977), or simply fulfilling a structural function (Lyons and Mosher 1987). However, these alternative functional roles have received little support or attention. In all, despite decades of research, abundant observational and experimental studies, and two crucial narrative reviews (Dubiec, Góźdź, and Mazgajski 2013; Scott-Baumann and Morgan 2015), we still do not know the overall adaptive significance of GNM, its generality across bird species, and the relative importance of each of the suggested mechanisms. Given that nest building is widespread across taxa —including but not limited to birds— and is increasingly recognized as an expression of the extended phenotype, a quantitative synthesis of this field is both timely and has potentially far-reaching implications.

To address this gap, we conducted a pre-registered systematic review and meta-analysis of experiments testing the effect of GNM on various fitness proxies across birds. Our meta-analysis explicitly evaluates the overall evidence for the adaptive significance of GNM, as well as assesses the relative importance of the main suggested hypotheses: the courtship hypothesis and parental care hypothesis (i.e., a combination of both the nest protection and drug hypotheses). In addition, we tested several potential moderators that might explain the observed context-dependency (i.e., high heterogeneity among effect sizes). Specifically, we tested whether the effect of GNM varied depending on the experimental design used (aromatic vs. non-aromatic vs. no added material), the timing of GNM addition (before hatching, after hatching, or continuously), the focal species studied, the type of fitness proxy investigated (e.g., physiology, morphology, reproduction), and the type of parasite (arthropods vs. microorganisms). Our meta-analysis of experimental studies is the first quantitative synthesis testing the causal relationship between GNM and fitness across birds.

We conducted a systematic search on Web of Science Core Collection and Scopus on the 7th of September, 2022 (see update below). The keyword search query was designed as a three-block query including terms related to our treatment of interest ((“green*” OR “herb*” OR “aromatic*”) AND “nest*”), population of interest (“bird*” OR “aves” OR “avian” OR “ornithol*” OR “passerine*” OR “passeriform*” OR “songbird*” OR list of all bird genera) and to limit the search results to experimental studies only (“experiment*” OR “manipulat*”). The complete search strings tailored to each specific platform are provided in Figure S5. Although our search string only included keywords in English, we planned to screen any study in English, Spanish, Portuguese and Hindi, but found none. We searched through titles, abstracts and keywords with no restrictions on publication date or study type. We validated our search using an initial library of 15 relevant articles that we had previously identified (Figure S3), ensuring all were retrieved. Additionally, we used the R package ‘litsearchr’ v1.0.0 (Grames et al. 2019), which did not yield any new relevant terms, confirm our queries. After removing 152 duplicates using the R package ‘revtools’ v0.4.1 (Westgate 2019), we identified 383 unique records.

Eligibility criteria

All titles and abstracts were equally allocated and screened independently by at least two screeners (SD, TR, JMGS) using the R package ‘revtools’ following a pre-designed decision tree with all inclusion/exclusion criteria (Figure S6). We included all studies on birds that experimentally manipulated the GNM (both aromatics or herbs). Forty articles passed the title-and-abstract screening and were further evaluated during the full-text screening, where each article was independently screened by a single screener (SD, TR, JMGS), with another screener verifying the procedure. We used the same criteria as above (Figure S6) with an additional evaluation of whether the variable of interest in the article was in our list of fitness proxies of interest (?@figsupp-fitness-proxies). Twenty-two articles passed the full-text screening, and were, thus, eligible for our meta-analysis. All conflicts in screening decisions (1% for title-and-abstract screening, and none for full-text screening) were resolved through discussion.

We updated our systematic literature search by repeating our search on the 12th of August, 2024, and found 53 unique articles after removing 13 duplicates. Only one observer (SD) performed the both screenings using the same decision trees as before (Figure S6), and found one additional eligible article for our meta-analysis. Lastly, we identified three additional eligible articles recommended by two colleagues. In all, 26 published articles were eligible for our meta-analysis, but we also obtained two additional unpublished studies (more details below and in Figure S7).

The data of each article were extracted evenly by one of three authors (SD, TR,  JMGS), with another screener verifying the procedure, and four authors (SD, TR, JMGS, AST) collectively discussing and revising unclear cases. We prioritised extracting primary data (i.e., measures of central tendency, variance and sample size) from text and tables, or, if that was not possible, from figures (e.g., boxplots) using the R package ‘metaDigitise’ v1.0.1 (Pick, Nakagawa, and Noble 2018). If primary data were not available, we extracted inferential statistics such as t and F values or descriptive statistics such as contingency tables (see below), that could be transformed into an effect size. Last, if the previous steps failed but the article had openly archived data, we calculated the means, standard deviation and sample sizes from them (n = 4).

We extracted the means, variances and sample sizes for each control-treatment pairwise comparison. Depending on the experimental design of each study, controls included non-aromatic materials such as grass or moss, lesser amounts of aromatic material than the treatments, no additional material added, or removal of material added by birds; whereas treatments consisted of aromatic materials added by authors, birds, or both. Those comparisons were coded either as non-aromatic GNM vs. aromatic GNM, or no added GNM vs. aromatic GNM, in our moderator “experimental design”. However, there was a third type of comparison used in some studies comparing nests with experimentally added non-aromatic GNM to nests without any added GNM or in which GNM added by the birds was removed by the authors. In this type of comparison, the non-aromatic GNM was considered the treatment, and we coded these 10 and 11 effect sizes, respectively, as non-aromatic GNM vs. no added GNM in our moderator “experimental design”. Importantly, our conclusions were robust to these effect sizes (@Table SX), so we kept them in our dataset, and the moderator “experimental design” was used for subsequent meta-regressions. In studies where three groups were compared (n = 5), we extracted all possible pairwise comparisons.

For each study, we also extracted information related to the study subjects (species of birds, geographical location of the study population), the methodology of the study (plant species used as treatment and control, fitness proxy measured) and the source from which we extracted data (e.g., figures, tables, raw data files, supplementary materials). We categorised the fitness proxies measured into 6 broad categories: physiology, morphology, reproduction, behaviour, parasite and pathogenic load, and phenology. For studies testing whether GNM affects the number of parasites or pathogens found in offspring and/or nests, we categorised them into arthropods or microorganisms. For a complete list of variables extracted, see Figure S2 . We did not include some traits reported in the original studies (k = 15 effect sizes, n = 5 studies) in our analyses because it was unclear from the study and our own judgement how they related to fitness (see ?@figsupp-excluded-proxies).

To obtain missing data (e.g., non-reported results, missing sample sizes), we contacted four corresponding authors. All four authors responded to our email, but only two provided the data as requested; one did not have the data any more, and one did not give a conclusive response. Additionally, to locate grey literature (i.e., unpublished data), we wrote to the corresponding author(s) of all the (n = 22 articles that passed full-text screening to ask for any potential unpublished data that could be included in our meta-analysis using a standardised email template (?@figsupp-email). One author provided data for an unpublished study on Blue Tits (see description in ?@figsupp-unpublished-study) and one of us (MO), provided data for an unpublished study on Common Buzzards (Buteo buteo).

Using the means, standard deviations and sample sizes for each control-treatment pairwise comparison, we calculated two effect sizes: a log response ratio (lnRR) and a standardised mean difference with heteroskedasticity correction (SMDH), using the escalc function (measure = ROM and SMDH respectively, vtype = “LS”) in the R package ‘metafor’ v4.8-0 (Viechtbauer 2010). We specified the treatment group as the numerator and the control group as the denominator for lnRR, and treatment minus control for SMDH, such that positive values indicate a ‘fitness’ increase in the treatment group. In cases such as measures of parasite load or prevalence, laying or hatching dates, courtship time, or scab score, the effect size sign was inverted prior to analysis to ensure that a positive value consistently meant an increase in fitness regardless of fitness proxy (i.e., to ensure comparability across effect sizes). SMDH could only be calculated when there were enough data points in both treatment and control groups to estimate the pooled variance; specifically, when the degrees of freedom (i.e., n1+n2−2) was greater than 1. There were nine effect sizes from one) study that did not fulfil that requirement, which we then excluded entirely from the dataset.

For the few cases for which we could not extract means and variances but inferential statistics such as t and F values, we estimated SMD values (k = 4, n = 2) using the equations provided by Cooper, Hedges, and Valentine (2019). For dichotomous variables (e.g., number of successful nests, k = 4, n = 2), we calculated the log odds ratio (lnOR) using the ‘escalc’ function (measure=OR) from ‘metafor’ and subsequently converted it to SMD using equations from Cooper, Hedges, and Valentine (2019). Since lnRR cannot be calculated from inferential statistics, or dichotomous variables, or for comparisons including zero or negative values (k = 7), the number of effect sizes included in the lnRR (k = 259, n = 28) subset is smaller than that of SMDH (k = 274, n = 28).

When calculating each effect size’s associated sampling variance, we prioritised the number of nests in each group over the number of individuals as the sample size whenever possible to reduce the effect of genetic relatedness non-independence. In cases where the same control or treatment group was used across multiple comparisons, sample sizes were adjusted prior to calculating effect sizes by dividing them by the number of times each group was used in a comparison to account for shared group non-independence. For cases where the authors of an original article reported a statistically non-significant difference between two groups but without providing numerical values (k = 16, n = 2), we assigned zero as the effect size for that comparison and imputed its sampling variance using the missing-case approach described in Nakagawa, Noble, et al. (2022) for lnRR and using \(\frac{n_1+n_2}{n_1×n_2}\) for SMD (Lajeunesse 2013).

Data analysis

We performed all analyses in R version 4.5.1 (R Core Team 2025). All multilevel meta-analytical models and meta-regressions were fitted using ‘metafor’ v.4.8-0 (Viechtbauer 2010). Sampling variances were modelled as variance-covariance matrices assuming a 0.5 correlation (ρ) between sampling variances from the same study (Noble et al. 2017) using the vcalc function from ‘metafor’ (See Supplementary Appendix Figure S8 for sensitivity analyses using different values of ρ).

We originally considered the following levels of non-independence for our analyses: multiple effect sizes collected from  (a) same study (Paper ID), (b) bird species (Species ID) and (c) geographical locations (population ID), (d) sub-populations within the same location (Experiment ID; e.g., birds studied at the same location for multiple years or birds studied at the same location separated by distance), (e) groups of individuals within a population (Group ID; e.g., males and females in a population, first clutch and second clutch) were represented in our data several times, and (f) repeated measurement of the same trait within the same individuals (Repeated trait ID; e.g., chick mass on day 7, 14 and 21).

Unless stated otherwise, we fitted the following random effects in all our models to account for non-independence: Paper ID, Group ID, Species ID, Population ID and an observation-level random effect (Observation ID). Our random effect structure deviated from our pre-registration since, initially, we had pre-registered the inclusion of Experiment ID and Repeated trait ID but not Population ID. However, Experiment ID and Group ID were almost indistinguishable, so we decided to leave out the one with lesser resolution, i.e. Experiment ID. Additionally, since few effect sizes were measured repeatedly, making Repeated trait ID almost identical to the observation-level random effect Observation ID, we excluded it (Figure S4). Last, we decided to add Population ID since we noticed after extracting the data but before any analyses that several studies were performed in the same geographical locations.

Meta-analytic mean and hypotheses

To estimate the effect of GNM on fitness across birds, we used two effect sizes: lnRR and SMD(H). Thus, throughout, we fitted two separate sets of model for each effect size. First, we fitted multilevel intercept-only meta-analytical model to test the overall effect, its uncertainty and heterogeneity. We used the rma.mv function from the ‘metafor’ package using the ‘REML’ method and a t distribution for calculating the test statistics and confidence intervals for the moderators. We also conducted a sensitivity analysis including some traits for which the relationship with fitness is unclear or debated in the literature (e.g., sex ratio, testosterone levels), to test the robustness of our results (Figure S9; k = 21 , n = 8). For all intercept-only models, we estimated the total amount of absolute heterogeneity (\(σ^2\); the total variation not explained by sampling error), its sources or relative heterogeneity (\(I^2\)), mean-standardised metric (CVH2) and variance-mean-standardised metric of heterogeneity (M2) following the pluralistic approach suggested by Yang, Noble, et al. (2024) .

To test the relative importance of the two main adaptive hypotheses suggested for GNM, that is, the courtship and the parental care hypothesis, which combines the nest protection and drug hypotheses, we ran a multilevel meta-regression including the moderator ‘hypothesis’ (levels: courtship, parental care, both). For this and all meta-regressions, we estimated \(R_{marginal}^2\),which corresponds to the percentage of heterogeneity explained by the moderators (Nakagawa and Schielzeth 2012). Unless stated otherwise, all meta-regressions correspond to unimoderator meta-regressions.

Publication Bias Test

Publication bias occurs when a subset of research findings, most often statistically non-significant ones, are less likely to be published or published later (Jennions et al. 2013).  There are two types of publication biases found to be commonplace in ecology and evolutionary biology: small-study and decline effects (Yang et al. 2023). We tested them for both lnRR and SMD(H) separately, following Nakagawa, Lagisz, et al. (2022). To test for small-study effects, we ran a multilevel meta-regression using the inverse of the effective sample size as the moderator. To test for decline effects (also known as time-lag bias; Sánchez-Tójar et al. (2018)), we ran a multilevel meta-regression using year of publication (mean-centered) as the moderator.

Both lnRR and SMD(H) intercept-only multilevel meta-analyses showed an average increase in fitness outcomes in nests with an experimental increase in GNM compared to control nests; however, the overall effect was only statistically significant for SMD(H).

First, lnRR showed an average statistically non-significant increase in fitness outcomes of 1.9% in nests with an experimental increase in GNM (Figure2 A; lnRR = 0.019, 95% CI: [-0.012 to 0.050], 95% PI: [-0.281 to 0.319], p-value = 0.223, k = 238, n = 26) but heterogeneity among effect sizes was high. Total raw heterogeneity (𝜎2) was 0.023 (Q = 2277.7, p < 0.001), the measures of relative (I2total = 98.6%) and standardised heterogeneity (CVH2total = 63.59 and M2total = 0.98) suggested high heterogeneity across effect sizes.

Second, SMD(H) showed an average statistically significant increase in fitness outcomes in nests with an experimental increase of GNM (Figure2 B; SMD(H) = 0.179, 95% CI: [0.048 to 0.310], 95% PI: [-0.829 to 1.188], p-value = 0.008, k = 253, n = 26), but heterogeneity among effect sizes was moderate to high. For lnRR, all three heterogeneity metrics exceeded the 75th percentile benchmark (see (supp-tab-heterogeneity?) for detailed estimates of heterogeneity).

These overall results, and their conclusions were robust to several sensitivity analyses. First, we fitted a pre-registered bivariate multilevel meta-analytic model including both effect sizes as response variables (lnRR = -0.015, p-value = 0.624 and SMD(H) = 0.250 , p-value = 0.069). In addition, we ran several sensitivity analyses to test the robustness of the overall results to: (i) the inclusion of traits for which their relationship with fitness was unclear (non-pre-registered), (ii) the removal effect sizes that were originally reported as simply statistically non-significant (non-pre-registered), (iii) both (i) and (ii) (non-pre-registered), and, (iv) the removal of effect sizes that were calculated by transforming inferential statistics. The number of effect sizes included in those sensitivity analyses ranged from 182 to 274 effect sizes, and the conclusions remained largely the same Table S2.

Discussion

Our pre-registered systematic review and meta-analysis provides the first quantitative synthesis of experimental studies on green nest material (GNM) across birds and reveals that, on average, supplementing nests with GNM yields only small benefits. The strength of the overall effects of GNM differed between the effect size metric used. Using the lnRR we found a non-significant 1.6% increase in overall fitness proxies, whereas the SMD(H) indicated a small but statistically significant positive effect (SMD(H)= 0.179). These differences in overall effect size magnitude likely reflect the underlying biological and statistical assumptions - lnRR incorporates the mean difference at a multiplicative scale whereas SMDH expresses the difference on an additive scale. There was moderate to high heterogeneity among the effect sizes, which we tried to understand using several biological and methodological moderators. The difference in relative heterogeneity (I2) between the two effect sizes aligns with broader trends observed in meta-analysis in ecology and evolution (Yang, Williams, et al. 2024).

Contrary to long-standing verbal models, neither the courtship hypothesis (visual/olfactory signalling) nor the parental-care hypothesis (parasite repellency or pharmacological “drug” effects) emerged as a consistent driver of these small gains. Effect estimates associated with courtship traits were positive but non-significant, and those associated with nestling protection were essentially zero. In other words, current evidence does not allow us to attribute the weak average benefit of GNM to a single prevailing mechanism. This result mirrors the idiosyncratic outcomes scattered through the primary literature and indicates that future tests will need to measure multiple pathways simultaneously—ideally pairing behavioural observations with direct assays of ectoparasites, microbial load, and nest-air volatiles.

Importantly, our results reveal that the commonly studied mechanistic hypotheses of GNM –whether through sexual signalling, parasite reduction, or direct nestling benefits– lack consistent empirical support across studies.

The discrepancy between metrics underscores how the inferred magnitude of benefit depends on the underlying scale: lnRR captures proportional shifts, whereas SMDᴴ reflects absolute shifts that are sensitive to among-study variance. Nonetheless, both converge on the same biological interpretation—if GNM is adaptive, the advantage appears subtle rather than dramatic, and it is expressed against a backdrop of substantial heterogeneity (I² ≈ 66–99 %).

Methodological context explained far more of the variance than biological hypothesis. Several practical recommendations follow. First, future experiments should adopt fully factorial designs that separate structure (any plant vs. none) from chemistry (aromatic vs. non-aromatic) and track volatilome dynamics across the nesting cycle. In summary, our review shows that adding green plant material to bird nests can increase fitness‐related measures, but the effect is small, context-dependent, and not yet pinned to a single mechanism. By refining experimental designs, broadening taxonomic coverage, and integrating chemical ecology with behavioural ecology, the next generation of studies can resolve whether GNM is truly an adaptive extended phenotype or merely a conspicuous but weak contributor to avian reproductive success.

When you ran these 2 meta-regressions specifically, you normally would run them twice, once without the intercept from which you obtain the values you are presenting (i.e., the real lnRR and SMD(H) estimates specific for each off the three levels: Parental Care, Courtship, and Both), and then you would run them with the intercept. In the latter, the intercept, unless you changed the order of the levels in the moderator (which is a factor), would represent “Both”, and the output would not tell you the difference between the estimate of level “Courtship” and “Both”, and “Parental Care” and “Both” (i.e., the estimate is a difference, e.g. lnRRboth = 0.025 - lnRRcourtship = 0.038 = -0.13) and whether that difference is statistically significant. We want to show whether any differences between the levels are statistically significant, so we should present these p-values to say whether each of the pair-wise comparisons differs statistically or not. For you to know if “Courtship” and “Parental Care”, you need to run an extra line of code, because from the model you only get the information about the difference between all levels of the factor (in this case, “Courtship” and “Parental Care”) and the reference level AKA intercept (in this case “Both”).
